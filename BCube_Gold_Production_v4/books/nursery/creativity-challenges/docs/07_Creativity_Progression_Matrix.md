# Creativity Progression Matrix

| Stage | Pages | Dominant thinking | Child action | Adult support | Evidence of progress |
|---|---:|---|---|---|---|
| Notice | 1–7 | Observational and imaginative | Identify, point, doodle, complete | Name details and ask open questions | Child notices visual possibilities |
| Try | 8–14 | Exploratory and constructive | Add, draw, combine and build | Model one option, then step back | Child attempts without needing one correct answer |
| Change | 15–21 | Flexible and divergent | Repeat, vary, recolour and rearrange | Ask, “What else could you try?” | Child produces a second variation |
| Express | 22–25 | Emotional and communicative | Choose, colour and describe | Invite simple reasons and preferences | Child explains a colour or design choice |
| Construct | 26–32 | Spatial and problem-solving | Assemble, repair, redesign and present | Break tasks into visible steps | Child combines parts into a whole |
| Reinterpret | 33–39 | Observational and transformative | Turn natural forms into designs | Compare real forms with invented forms | Child transforms a familiar form |
| Invent | 40–43 | Divergent and purposeful | Design, imagine, solve and narrate | Encourage unusual but understandable ideas | Child creates and explains an original idea |
| Celebrate | 44 | Reflective and confident | Select, share and celebrate | Praise effort, choices and persistence | Child identifies creative growth |

## Fine-motor progression
Broad crayon marks → controlled lines → enclosed colouring → shape assembly → visual sequencing → detailed additions.

## Language progression
Name → choose → describe → compare → explain → present.

## Assessment principle
Assess engagement, flexibility, persistence and communication—not artistic realism or uniformity.
